## [Structure] Sentence Patterns

---

**Front:**

You want to acknowledge that a benefit is real before explaining a clear
limitation. Produce a balanced concession sentence that emphasises the benefit
with `do` or `does`.

**Back:**

**Although [X] does [provide a benefit], [limitation].**

Structure: `Although + subject + do/does + base verb + benefit, + limitation.`

Example: Although online education does improve access, many students struggle
without face-to-face support.

Source:
`10_knowledge_base/sentence_patterns/archive/although-x-does-y-limitation.md`

**Front:**

You are concluding an advantages–disadvantages essay. You accept that some
advantages exist, but judge that they are not enough to outweigh the costs.
Produce the final-position pattern.

**Back:**

**In my view, despite [advantages], these do not compensate for [costs or
damage].**

Structure: `In my view, despite + noun phrase describing advantages, these do not
compensate for + noun phrase describing costs or damage.`

Example: In my view, despite the convenience offered by private cars, these
benefits do not compensate for the resulting congestion and air pollution.

Source:
`10_knowledge_base/sentence_patterns/archive/despite-advantages-do-not-compensate-for-costs.md`

**Front:**

You want to show that an activity or opportunity was once limited to a privileged
group but is now available to a wider group. Produce the two-sentence historical
comparison pattern.

**Back:**

**[X] used to be the preserve of [privileged group]. Now even [wider group] have
access to [X].**

Structure: `[Activity, resource, or opportunity] + used to be the preserve of +
[privileged group]. Now even + [wider group] + have access to + [it/them].`

Example: International education used to be the preserve of wealthy families. Now
even students on modest incomes have access to overseas study programmes.

`10_knowledge_base/sentence_patterns/archive/from-exclusive-to-accessible.md`

**Front:**

You want to show that a resource or benefit is shared unequally: a small group
receives most of it while the larger group receives less. Produce the full
contrast pattern.

**Back:**

**[Resource or benefit] is unevenly distributed, with a small number of [group]
[receiving most], while the majority [receive less].**

Structure: `[Resource/benefit] + is unevenly distributed, with + a small number of
[group] + [benefiting], while + the majority + [receive less].`

Example: The benefits of automation are unevenly distributed, with a small number
of technology companies earning enormous profits, while the majority of displaced
workers receive little support.

## [Expression] Collocations and Chunks

---

**Front:**

Culture, information, or entertainment has been simplified and reshaped into a
product for a particular audience. Produce the formal critical expression.

**Back:**

**be packaged for [audience] consumption**

Function: Describe how something is adapted and presented as a product for a
particular audience.

Example: Religious ceremonies may lose their original meaning when they are
packaged for tourist consumption.

Common collocations: be packaged for tourist consumption; be packaged for mass
consumption; be packaged for public consumption.

Source:
`10_knowledge_base/expressions/archive/be-packaged-for-consumption.md`

---

**Front:**

You want to explain that revenue or profit stays in a community and continues to
benefit its businesses and residents. Produce the formal passive expression.

**Back:**

**be retained within the local economy**

Function: Describe money or profit that remains in a community and benefits local
businesses and residents.

Example: Governments can support independent businesses to ensure that more
tourism revenue is retained within the local economy.

Common collocations: revenue retained within the local economy; profits retained
within the local economy; keep tourist spending within the local economy.

Source:
`10_knowledge_base/expressions/archive/be-retained-within-the-local-economy.md`

**Front:**

You want to emphasise serious harm while focusing on the damaged result rather
than the person or thing that caused it. Produce the passive expression.

**Back:**

**be severely damaged**

Function: Describe serious physical, environmental, economic, or reputational
harm.

Example: Fragile ecosystems can be severely damaged by uncontrolled tourism.

Common collocations: be severely damaged by pollution; be severely damaged by mass
tourism; severely damaged infrastructure.

Source:
`10_knowledge_base/expressions/archive/be-severely-damaged.md`

**Front:**

Money, resources, opportunities, or benefits are not shared equally across people,
groups, or regions. Produce the formal passive expression.

**Back:**

**be unevenly distributed**

Function: Describe something that is not shared equally across people, groups, or
regions.

Example: The economic benefits of urban development are often unevenly
distributed.

Common collocations: income is unevenly distributed; benefits are unevenly
distributed; opportunities are unevenly distributed.

Source:
`10_knowledge_base/expressions/archive/be-unevenly-distributed.md`

**Front:**

You want to refer concisely to international visitors and the countries that
receive them when discussing shared benefits, costs, or responsibilities. Produce
the paired expression.

**Back:**

**both tourists and host countries**

Function: Refer concisely to the two main groups affected by international
tourism.

Example: Sustainable tourism can create long-term benefits for both tourists and
host countries.

Common collocations: benefits for both tourists and host countries; be beneficial
to both tourists and host countries; effects on both tourists and host countries.

**Front:**

An activity is one of several causes that increases the amount of heat-trapping
gases released into the atmosphere. Produce the measured causal expression.

**Back:**

**contribute to greenhouse gas emissions**

Function: Explain that an activity is one of several causes of emissions that
drive climate change.

Example: Frequent air travel contributes significantly to global greenhouse gas
emissions.

Common collocations: contribute significantly to greenhouse gas emissions;
transport-related greenhouse gas emissions; reduce greenhouse gas emissions.

Source:
`10_knowledge_base/expressions/archive/contribute-to-greenhouse-gas-emissions.md`

Common collocations: have a greater awareness of cultural differences; have a

Common collocations: precarious employment; precarious work; seasonal and

You want to refer collectively and considerately to people who have relatively

**Back:**

**the less well-off**
