## Transcript (verbatim)

So let's talk about the first thing that lowers your score in the exam.

So many students try to prepare for writing by learning long lists of complex academic words, and then they try and include these words in their essays.

The problem with this is that this leads to candidates using words that are inappropriate or wrong as well.

Now there is always a problem if you try and communicate above your natural level, and I'm going to show you what that looks like to an examiner.

So if we have a look at this example in the mild weather, the scorching sun can cause a dilapidated impacts for the people of walking. The climate for a few hours is difficult for them to sweating because of the sun.

Now there's a few problems here. You cannot have scorching sun in mild weather, because scorching means that it's so hot that it is burning your skin, so that sentence doesn't make any sense.

Now, the candidate wanted to use a higher level word. They wanted to sort of force some vocabulary into their paragraph. So they said they wrote scorching instead of hot.

Same problem with the mild weather example. They didn't just want to say it was cold, they wanted to say it was mild. And so they got the meaning of the word wrong.

Now they probably looked in a dictionary and thought, this is a high level word, dilapidated. And they wanted to put dilapidated into their writing. So they looked for another way to say bad, and they found the word dilapidated and so they, they didn't want to say bad impact. So what they did is they wrote dilapidated impact. Now, unfortunately, that doesn't make any sense at all in English. It doesn't collocate with the word impacts.

And um, and then again, the climate for a few hours also doesn't make sense because climate is for a region or area on Earth, and it doesn't mean that it's the change in weather for a few hours.
