# Idea: Mandatory proposals often fail on enforcement

## Theme
government-society

## Learning Status
New

## Core Idea
Proposing that something become compulsory sounds appealing in principle, but in
practice it often collapses on enforcement: there's rarely a clear, fair standard for
who must comply, who is exempt, or how non-compliance should be punished.

## Skeleton

| Step | Content |
|---|---|
| Claim | Turning a good idea into a mandatory requirement is often impractical, even when the underlying idea has merit |
| Reason | "Mandatory" requires clear, enforceable, fair standards — but many proposals don't actually have consensus on what the standard should be |
| Mechanism | Once you ask who would be exempt, who would be punished for non-compliance, and by what standard compliance is judged, the proposal runs into problems that were invisible when it was just an abstract suggestion |
| Impact | The proposal remains attractive in theory but becomes unworkable once enforcement is considered — a well-intentioned idea, not a viable policy |
| Example | Requiring all parents to take a parenting course: no agreed definition of "good parenting" to build a course around, no fair way to punish non-attendance, no way to handle parents with different schedules/circumstances |

## Reusable IELTS Angle
Standard rebuttal to any essay proposing that something "should be required/
compulsory/mandatory" — works whenever the proposal sounds reasonable in principle
but the essay wants to argue it's impractical.

## Speaking Use
Part 3 answers to "Should [X] be made compulsory?": "It sounds like a good idea in
theory, but I think it would be hard to enforce fairly — who decides the standard,
and what happens to people who don't comply?"
