# W001 — `-ed` vs `-ing` Adjectives

## Core Rule

- **`-ed` adjective** = how a **person feels** (the receiver of the effect)
- **`-ing` adjective** = what **causes** the feeling (the source of the effect)

> `I was bored.` (I felt bored — receiver)
> `The lecture was boring.` (the lecture caused boredom — source)

## Why it matters in IELTS

This error appears in speaking when describing experiences, topics, and people. `an amused story` vs `an amusing story` is the classic example. Mixing these up affects both LR and GRA.

## Common mistakes

❌ `It was an amused story.` (the story cannot feel amused)
❌ `She was amazing at the result.` (she cannot BE amazing; she IS amazed)
❌ `The topic was very interested.`

## Good examples

✅ `It was an amusing story.` (the story CAUSES amusement)
✅ `She was amazed at the result.` (she FEELS amazement)
✅ `The topic was very interesting.` (the topic CAUSES interest)
✅ `I was interested in the topic.` (I FEEL interest)

## Reusable patterns

| `-ing` (source/cause) | `-ed` (feeling/receiver) |
|---|---|
| interesting | interested |
| boring | bored |
| exciting | excited |
| surprising | surprised |
| amusing | amused |
| tiring | tired |
| confusing | confused |
| satisfying | satisfied |
| motivating | motivated |

**Quick test:** Ask "Who/what has the feeling?"
→ If it's a **person**: `-ed`
→ If it's a **thing/situation**: `-ing`

## Mini practice

Choose the correct form:

1. `The documentary was really (interested/interesting).`
2. `I was (surprised/surprising) by the news.`
3. `He found the lecture very (bored/boring).`

**Answers:**
1. **interesting** (the documentary = source)
2. **surprised** (I = receiver)
3. **boring** (the lecture = source)
