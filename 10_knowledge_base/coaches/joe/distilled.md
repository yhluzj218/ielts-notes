## Teaching Philosophy

Joe's primary frame is natural communication over exam mechanics. He consistently redirects Connie away from frameworks, scripted answers, and vocabulary anxiety — toward relaxed, genuine, fluent speaking. His core message: Connie's English is already strong; the bottleneck is overthinking.

**This same frame carries over to his Writing course (added 2026-07-02):** the IELTS Writing exam exists only to test communication ability — "we don't care about how complicated it is, we care about how easy it is to understand." Complexity, advanced vocabulary, and memorised structures are not rewarded in themselves; they are actively penalised when they interfere with clear communication. Same philosophy, two skills.

## Writing — What Examiners Care About (Band Descriptors Overview, added 2026-07-02)

Source: `originals/writing-band-descriptors-overview_2026-07-02.md`

| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| Core principle | The Writing exam has exactly one purpose: test whether you can communicate in English so a reader can understand you. Not how complex the writing is — how easy it is to understand. |
| Four official criteria (as Joe frames them) | **Task Achievement** — readable, paragraphed, clear ideas, arguments actually communicated. **Coherence & Cohesion** — sentence/grammar/punctuation/paragraph connection of ideas. **Lexical Resource** — words used with correct meaning/definition, effective for the intended communication. **Grammar** — does the grammar help the reader understand actions and connections between ideas. |
| Rule for evaluating any technique | If it doesn't improve one of these four band-descriptor areas, it does not improve your score — regardless of how advanced or clever it looks. |
| Explicit examples given | Memorising sentences and complex sentences (for their own sake) are both named directly in the official band descriptors as things that hurt score, not help it. |

### Detail — #1 Forcing Vocabulary (added 2026-07-02)

Source: `originals/writing-mistake-01-forcing-vocabulary_2026-07-02.md`

| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| Principle | Do not reach for higher-register words from a word list. Communicating above your natural level produces wrong collocations, wrong register, and nonsensical sentences — this lowers score, it does not raise it. |
| Mechanism (why it fails) | 1) Wrong meaning/register — e.g. "scorching" (extreme heat) used to mean "hot" in "mild weather" — contradiction. 2) Wrong collocation — "dilapidated impact" instead of "bad/negative impact"; "dilapidated" doesn't collocate with "impact" at all. 3) Wrong sense of a word — "climate" (region-level, long-term) used to mean short-term weather ("climate for a few hours"). |
| Rule for learning a new word | Before using a word in the exam: know its word type/form, its collocations, and its precise meaning — not just a dictionary synonym. |
| Reusable? | Yes — directly usable as an editing checklist question: "did I pick this word because it's accurate, or because it sounds advanced?" |

**Confirms existing project rule:** matches `CLAUDE.md` 核心規則 #4 (Connie 不缺詞彙，不要導向背單字/用更高級詞彙) and `10_knowledge_base/consensus/writing.md` § 5 "Vocabulary Is Not the Priority". This is not new direction — it's Joe independently confirming the same rule with a concrete mechanism. See consensus file, updated with this evidence.

**⭐ Refinement from a live lesson (2026-07-02, 1:1 writing lesson — see `01_lessons/writing/writing-lesson_2026-07-02.md`):** Connie asked directly whether an advanced word is allowed if it genuinely fits and comes to mind naturally (not from a memorised list). Joe's answer clarifies the rule is not "never use advanced words":

> "That's fine. The problem comes when you try to make your writing more complicated [on purpose]. That's when mistakes happen. When you stop using words you naturally know and start using words a teacher gave you in a list or that you memorised — that's when problems start. If you communicate naturally and simply, there's no problem."

This is an important refinement for Connie specifically: the anti-pattern is *forcing* unfamiliar words to sound impressive, not using a genuinely known advanced word that surfaces naturally. Directly relevant given Connie 不缺詞彙 — she should not hold back a natural, correct advanced word out of fear of triggering this anti-pattern.

**⭐ Further confirmation from the video course's own Vocabulary section (2026-07-02 — see `originals/writing-vocabulary-natural-level_2026-07-02.md`):** despite opening that section with "learning more vocabulary can improve your score" (`writing-vocabulary-intro_2026-07-02.md`), Joe's actual conclusion circles back to the same rule:

> "Don't try and communicate above your natural level because this leads to lots of cohesion problems... Native speakers only use very complex vocabulary when they are seeking to communicate a very exact meaning about something complicated... use vocabulary that is natural. Do not attempt to upgrade."

New mechanism added: mismatched register creates **Coherence & Cohesion** problems, not just Lexical Resource ones — disjointed writing where complex phrases sit next to natural ones reads as inconsistent, which directly lowers score. New example given: George Orwell's *Animal Farm* — great literature often uses simple vocabulary effectively; imitating complex native-speaker writing is not the goal.

**⭐ Synonym precision — new detail (2026-07-02 — see `originals/writing-synonyms_2026-07-02.md`):** Even a "correct" natural-level word can be wrong if it's the wrong synonym for the context. Two words can share a rough dictionary meaning but differ in collocation, connotation, or scope:

| Pair | Trap | Rule |
|---|---|---|
| huge/enormous vs vast | "a huge/enormous pay rise" ✅ — "a vast pay rise" ❌ | `vast` = large open space; doesn't collocate with `pay` |
| single-minded vs stubborn | Similar meaning, different connotation | `stubborn` skews negative; `single-minded` can be positive or negative depending on context |
| uninhabited (islands) vs "uninhabited seats" | "uninhabited islands" ✅ — "uninhabited seats" ❌ (should be empty/vacant/no seats available) | `uninhabited` only applies to a geographical place with no people living there, not to seats |
| come to blows vs "come to punches" | "come to blows" ✅ (fixed idiom) — "come to punches" ❌ | Fixed collocation — doesn't extend to related nouns |
| assistance vs help | "Can I be of assistance?" (formal, e.g. hotel) vs "Can I help you?" (informal, e.g. friend) | Match formality to context |
| trek vs walk | "I trek to the park every day" ❌ if the park is close | `trek` implies difficult, long-distance travel |
| commences vs starts | Formal vs informal register pair | English has no separate grammatical formal/informal form (unlike many other languages) — register is signalled entirely through word choice |

**⚠️ Directly named as a personal risk for Connie:** Joe explicitly says this mistake is especially common for Mandarin (and Japanese/Vietnamese) L1 speakers — teachers translate synonyms as equivalent to one L1 word, but the English words differ in collocation/connotation/register. This deepens the existing "Rule for learning a new word" from Mistake #1: don't just learn a word's dictionary meaning — check its collocations, connotation, and register before using it, especially for words that feel like straightforward translations from Mandarin.

### Detail — #2 Using a Template (added 2026-07-02)

Source: `originals/writing-mistake-02-using-a-template_2026-07-02.md`

| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| Principle | Do not use fixed, memorised templates (e.g. fill-in-the-gap templates from social media teachers). Examiners recognise repeated templates; grammar visibly breaks down at the seam between memorised chunk and own content; any template-based writing is capped at Band 2. |
| Mechanism (why it fails) | Templates are written for generic use, not by native speakers with reliable grammar; ideas and wording don't actually link to the specific question; examiners see the same templates repeatedly and mark them down as a pattern. |
| Rule | If you can write English, just write your own English — don't insert a template. If your English is weak, writing your own (even imperfect) English still scores higher than a template, because template writing is capped at Band 2 regardless of quality. |

**⚠️ Potential terminology conflict — flagged, mostly resolved by new evidence, final confirmation still needed:**

This project's `10_knowledge_base/writing/writing_patterns.md` § 5 and § 7 are literally named "Introduction Templates" and "Conclusion Templates" — fixed sentence frames with slots, e.g. `[Paraphrase of topic]. [This essay will argue that + your position + one-line reason.]`. Joe's warning is aimed at fully memorised generic filler (his description: "memorize these words... put what you think in the gaps"), which sounds close to the same mechanism.

Distinction as currently understood:
- Project's "templates" = functional skeleton — only the linking phrase is fixed; the position, topic, and content inside the slot must be freshly paraphrased per essay.
- Joe's "template" = fully generic, reused near-verbatim across many candidates' essays, including content-adjacent phrasing — the whole thing is memorised, not just a connector.

**⭐ Strong supporting evidence (2026-07-02, live 1:1 lesson, high-confidence transcript):** Joe personally taught/dictated this exact sentence in class: `"This essay argues that these changes have been transformative because consumers can buy things from other countries and sell things online."` — structurally identical to the project's own Introduction Template. Joe teaching this himself, right after explicitly warning against memorised templates, strongly supports that his warning targets fully-generic social-media templates, not connector-only skeleton frames. See `01_lessons/writing/writing-lesson_2026-07-02.md`.

Logged to `10_knowledge_base/conflicts.md` per ADR-007 — leaning resolved, but not auto-closed without Connie's confirmation.
