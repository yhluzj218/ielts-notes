## 1. Writing Process (Fixed)

Always follow this sequence before writing any essay:

```
Read question
    ↓
Identify question TYPE
    ↓
Choose STRUCTURE
    ↓
Write SKELETON (4 parts)
    ↓
Write ESSAY
```

**Why this matters:** Skipping any step causes downstream problems.
Jumping to content before confirming type leads to off-topic essays (TR penalty).
Jumping to essay before skeleton leads to underdeveloped paragraphs.

---

## 2. Skeleton Principle

A skeleton is not a draft. Its purpose is to decide:

- **Position** — what you will argue
- **Main ideas** — what each body paragraph will say
- **Paragraph functions** — what job each paragraph does

Do not write examples, reasons, or details in the skeleton.
A skeleton is a structure plan, not a content plan.

**Skeleton format (4 parts):**

```
Introduction: paraphrase + position / task statement
Body 1: topic sentence (one clear main idea)
Body 2: topic sentence (one clear main idea)
Conclusion: restate position (one sentence)
```

**Self-check before moving to essay:**

- [ ] Does the introduction signal what the essay will cover?
  - Problem+Solution: does it name BOTH the reasons AND the solutions? (not just "I will show...")
- [ ] Does Body 1 have only one main idea?
- [ ] Does Body 2 have a different main idea from Body 1?
- [ ] Does the conclusion restate — not add new points?
  - Comparing short-term vs long-term in conclusion is valid and shows mature thinking.

## 3. Question Type Identification

Always read the final instruction sentence first — not the topic content.

| Instruction keywords | Question type |
|---|---|
| `What's your opinion?` · `Do you agree?` · `Is it good or bad?` · `Is it positive or negative?` · `Do advantages outweigh disadvantages?` | **Agree / Disagree (Opinion)** |
| `Discuss both views` · `Some people think A... others think B... discuss` | **Discuss Both Views + Opinion** |
| `What are the reasons?` + `What solutions...` / `How can...` | **Problem + Solution** |
| Two separate questions with different focuses (Why...? + Is it...? / To what extent...?) | **Two-part Question** |

**Common trap:** If the topic has two sides (good/bad, positive/negative) but the instruction only asks for YOUR view → it is Agree/Disagree, not Discuss Both Views.

**Decision rule:** Classify by what the instruction asks you to DO, not by the topic content.

## 4. Essay Structures by Question Type

> 詳細 Skeleton 模板與真實例子 → [`skeletons.md`](skeletons.md)
> 下方是快速結構摘要；寫文章前建議直接看 skeletons.md。

### 4.1 Agree / Disagree (Opinion Essay)

```
Introduction: paraphrase + clear position (agree or disagree)
Body 1: strongest reason supporting your position + development
Body 2: second reason OR acknowledge opposing view + refute
Conclusion: restate position
```

**Position rule:** State your position in the introduction. Do not sit on the fence unless the question explicitly asks for a balanced view.

---

### 4.2 Discuss Both Views + Opinion

```
Introduction: paraphrase + signal you will discuss both views
Body 1: first view (argued fairly — even if you disagree with it)
Body 2: second view (your preferred view)
Conclusion: restate your overall position
```

**Key rule (Matt):** Body 1 must argue the opposing view as if you believe it. A dismissive paragraph signals inability to engage — examiner penalises this.

---

### 4.3 Problem + Solution

**Recommended structure (paired):**

```
Introduction: paraphrase problem + signal you will give reasons and solutions
Body 1: Reason 1 → Solution 1
Body 2: Reason 2 → Solution 2
Conclusion: restate overall point
```

**Why paired over separate:** Each cause is immediately linked to its solution. Easier for the reader to follow. Avoids the "Solution B solves which Reason?" disconnect.

**Alternative structure (when solutions address all causes together):**

```
Introduction
Body 1: Reasons (2 causes)
Body 2: Solutions (2 solutions)
Conclusion
```

Use this only when both solutions genuinely address both causes.

---

### 4.4 Two-part Question

```
Introduction: paraphrase + signal you will answer both questions
Body 1: Full answer to Question 1
Body 2: Full answer to Question 2
Conclusion: restate overall point
```

**Key rule:** Both questions must be answered with equal depth. Leaving one short causes Task Response penalty.

## 5. Introduction Templates

> 完整句型庫（依 Skeleton 位置分類）→ [`sentence_patterns.md`](sentence_patterns.md)

### Opinion Essay

> `[Paraphrase of topic].` `[This essay will argue that + your position + one-line reason.]`

Example:
> "While some argue that family businesses offer unique advantages, this essay will argue that the drawbacks tend to outweigh the benefits for most families."

**⚠️ What NOT to write (Joe, writing lesson, 2026-07-02):** Do not open with a pure paraphrase of the question as your entire first sentence — this is a common technique taught in China but caps your score around Band 7 even done perfectly, because it shows minimal written communication ability (too simplistic — anyone can do it, even someone who doesn't really speak English).

**Alternative opening — "then vs now" narrative contrast (Joe, 2026-07-02):** Instead of paraphrasing, contrast historical/past practice with the present.

Example (from class):
> "A hundred years ago, if you wanted to buy something, you would need to go to a local shop, and if you wanted to buy something in particular you might need to drive to many shops. Nowadays, the way we purchase things has changed dramatically and we can buy whatever we want, just by clicking a button on a smart phone."

Then state position with the same functional connector: `This essay argues that these changes have been transformative because [reason 1] and [reason 2].`

---

### Problem + Solution

> `[Paraphrase of problem].` `This essay will explore the main reasons behind this trend and suggest several measures to address it.`

Example:
> "Excessive clothing consumption has become a significant issue in many societies. This essay will explain the key causes of this behaviour and propose effective ways to reduce it."

**What NOT to write:**
> ~~"I will show some reasons and solutions in the following paragraph."~~
> (Too vague — does not signal both questions are answered)

---

### Discuss Both Views

> `[Paraphrase of both views].` `This essay will examine both perspectives before presenting my own view.`

## 6. Body Paragraph Development Chain

Required chain for every body paragraph (Matt's rule):

```
[Point]    — state the main idea
[Explain]  — why / how
[Support]  — reason / example / specific detail
[Result]   — consequence / what follows
[Complete] — optional: close or extend the argument
```

After writing each paragraph, label every sentence with its function.

**Self-check:**
- Does every sentence add something new?
- Are any two sentences doing the same job? → Remove the weaker one
- Does the chain reach [Result] level?

---

## 7. Conclusion Templates

**Restate position — do not add new points.**

> "In conclusion, [restate your main position in different words]. [Optional: one-sentence long-term / wider implication.]"

**Double conjunction trap (G010):**
> ~~"Although X, however Y"~~ → Write `Although X, Y` OR `X. However, Y.` Never both.

## 8. Common Argument Frameworks

Use these when building Body paragraph ideas. Do not force one if it does not fit the topic.

| Framework | When to use |
|---|---|
| Individual vs Society | Who is responsible? Who benefits / suffers? |
| Short-term vs Long-term | Immediate effect vs lasting impact |
| Economic vs Environmental | Cost / profit vs ecological impact |
| Government vs Individual | Who should act? Policy vs personal responsibility |
| Developed vs Developing countries | Global inequality angle |
