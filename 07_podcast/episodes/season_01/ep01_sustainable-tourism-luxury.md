## Short Article

### When Sustainable Tourism Becomes a Luxury Product

International tourism is often presented as a choice between economic growth and
environmental protection. In reality, the debate is more complicated. Travel can
create employment, support cultural exchange, and give visitors a greater awareness
of communities different from their own. At the same time, flights produce
greenhouse gas emissions, popular destinations become overcrowded, and local
traditions may be repackaged for visitors. The newest answer to these problems is
“sustainable tourism”: smaller hotels, slower journeys, local food, ethical wildlife
experiences, and longer stays.

The idea is attractive, but the way it is marketed creates a serious contradiction.
Many sustainable travel packages are significantly more expensive than ordinary
holidays. A short eco-retreat can cost more than a week at a conventional resort,
while low-carbon rail journeys are sometimes less convenient and more expensive
than flying. Although these products do encourage travellers to think about their
impact, they can make responsible tourism look like a lifestyle available only to
wealthy consumers.

This matters because environmental responsibility cannot depend entirely on
individual purchasing power. If sustainable choices are aimed mainly at affluent
travellers, the less well-off face an unfair choice: continue using cheaper,
less sustainable services or stop travelling altogether. Moral language can make
this inequality worse. A company may praise wealthy customers for “travelling
consciously” while ignoring the fact that many families simply cannot afford the
premium price.

Multinational corporations complicate the issue further. Large hotel groups,
airlines, and booking platforms can bring sustainable practices to millions of
customers. They can reduce plastic use, improve energy efficiency, and make
environmental information visible during booking. Although their scale does create
opportunities for rapid change, their commercial interests may weaken the meaning of
the campaign. A hotel chain might invite guests to reuse towels while continuing to
build water-intensive resorts in drought-prone regions. An airline might advertise
carbon offsets while encouraging customers to take more frequent flights.

There is also an economic question. Tourism revenue is often unevenly distributed.
Large businesses may capture most of the profit, while local workers receive low
wages and insecure seasonal employment. Labelling a holiday “sustainable” does not
automatically change who owns the hotels, who controls the booking platform, or
where the profits go. A beautifully designed eco-brand can therefore hide a
conventional business model.

However, rejecting sustainable tourism would be a mistake. The concept can still
produce meaningful improvements if it becomes more practical and inclusive.
Governments can improve rail networks, regulate environmental claims, and support
locally owned businesses. Travel companies can offer affordable low-impact options
rather than treating sustainability as a luxury upgrade. Tourists can travel less
frequently, stay longer, use local services, and question vague green promises.

The real goal should not be to make every traveller feel guilty. It should be to
make responsible choices normal, affordable, and easy to verify. Although
sustainable tourism does raise awareness, awareness alone cannot transform an
industry. Lasting change requires prices, infrastructure, ownership, and regulation
to move in the same direction. Otherwise, sustainability risks becoming a reassuring
label for consumers rather than a practical system capable of reducing harm at
scale.

## Key Issue

- Does sustainable tourism reduce harm, or merely give expensive travel a greener
  image?
- Is it fair to place environmental responsibility on travellers who have limited
  budgets?
- Can multinational corporations produce genuine reform while encouraging more
  consumption?
- Who receives the economic benefits of sustainable tourism?
- Should governments make low-impact travel cheaper and easier?

## Useful IELTS Ideas

- Tourism promotes cultural awareness, but cultural exchange does not cancel out
  environmental damage.
- Sustainable choices have limited impact when only affluent consumers can afford
  them.
- Individual responsibility is ineffective without affordable infrastructure and
  credible regulation.
- Multinational corporations can spread better practices quickly because of their
  scale.
- Corporate sustainability campaigns may become greenwashing when the wider
  business model remains unchanged.
- Tourism revenue can grow while remaining unevenly distributed among local people.
- Supporting locally owned businesses helps retain more income within the local
  economy.
- Responsible travel should be normal and accessible rather than marketed as a
  luxury identity.

## Target Pattern

**Pattern:** `Although [X] does [provide a benefit], [limitation].`

Use this pattern to acknowledge that a benefit is genuine before identifying its
limit. The auxiliary `does` adds emphasis. Do not add `however` to the same sentence.

## Target Vocabulary

### have a greater awareness of

- Meaning: understand or recognise an issue more clearly than before
- Natural usage: useful for changes in public understanding, cultural knowledge, or
  environmental concern
- Examples:
  - Travellers now have a greater awareness of their environmental impact.
  - Consumers have a greater awareness of greenwashing than they did a decade ago.
  - Students should develop a greater awareness of cultural differences.
- Common mistake: use `awareness of`, not `awareness about`, in this expression

### multinational corporations

- Meaning: large companies that operate in more than one country
- Natural usage: useful for globalisation, employment, taxation, investment, and
  profit leakage
- Examples:
  - Multinational corporations can introduce common standards across many markets.
  - Local businesses often struggle to compete with multinational corporations.
  - Governments should require multinational corporations to report environmental
    impacts clearly.
- Common mistake: do not assume the term is automatically negative; explain the
  specific benefit or harm

### the less well-off

- Meaning: people with relatively limited financial resources
- Natural usage: a neutral, considerate alternative to `the poor`
- Examples:
  - Higher prices may exclude the less well-off from sustainable travel.
  - Public transport is especially important for the less well-off.
  - Subsidies can make cultural activities accessible to the less well-off.
- Common mistake: use it only for financial circumstances, not every form of
  disadvantage

The Podcast has two hosts:

  numbers that do not add up. She is especially interested in whether ordinary
  people can actually use a proposed solution.
- Emily is quick, confident, and commercially aware. She notices branding,
  efficiency, reach, and business incentives first. She can defend a position
  strongly, but she listens and is willing to revise it.

They are friends and work partners, not enemies. Let them interrupt naturally, ask
follow-up questions, challenge weak reasoning, concede good points, correct
themselves, and occasionally use light humour. Their exchange should sound
spontaneous rather than memorised.

Learning target:

`Although [X] does [provide a benefit], [limitation].`

# ElevenLabs Speaking Practice Script

Welcome back, Connie.

Today, we're practising one useful IELTS sentence pattern:

Although something does provide a benefit, there is still a limitation.

That's all we're focusing on today. No grammar lesson. Just speaking.

Ready?

Listen first.

Although sustainable tourism does raise awareness, it can remain unaffordable for
the less well-off.

Now, repeat after me.

Although sustainable tourism does raise awareness, it can remain unaffordable for
the less well-off.

[pause 5 seconds]

Good. Again.

Although sustainable tourism does raise awareness, it can remain unaffordable for
the less well-off.

[pause 5 seconds]

Nice. Now it's your turn to use the same pattern in different situations.

Let's start with education.

Can you make one sentence using today's pattern?

Think of one real benefit of online education, and then give one limitation.

Take your time.

[pause 10 seconds]

Here's one possible answer.

Although online education does improve access, some students still struggle without
face-to-face support.

Good. Now let's try technology.

Think about artificial intelligence. Give me one benefit and one limitation.

Take your time.

[pause 10 seconds]

Here's one possible answer.

Although artificial intelligence does improve efficiency, it may weaken independent
thinking.

Great. Let's move to the environment.

Think about electric cars. What do they improve, and what problem remains?

Take your time.

[pause 10 seconds]

Here's one possible answer.

Although electric cars do reduce urban air pollution, their batteries still require
resource-intensive production.

Nice. Now let's try the workplace.

Think about remote work. Give me one genuine advantage and one limitation.

Take your time.

[pause 10 seconds]

Here's one possible answer.

Although remote work does offer greater flexibility, some employees feel socially
isolated.

Excellent. One final topic: tourism.

Think about multinational corporations in the tourism industry. What benefit do they
bring, and what is the limitation?

Take your time.

[pause 10 seconds]

Here's one possible answer.

Although multinational corporations do create employment, many of the jobs are
seasonal and precarious.

Good work.

Let's finish by saying today's key sentence one more time.

Although sustainable tourism does raise awareness, it can remain unaffordable for
the less well-off.

[pause 5 seconds]

Excellent work today.

You used one sentence pattern across five different IELTS topics.

Keep practising it until the structure feels automatic.
