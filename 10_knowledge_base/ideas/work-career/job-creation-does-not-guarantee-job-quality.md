# Idea: Job creation does not guarantee job quality

## Theme
work-career

## Learning Status
New

## Core Idea
An industry or policy may genuinely create jobs, but the jobs themselves can be
unstable, seasonal, or low-paid — so a growth in the number of jobs doesn't mean the
jobs are actually good.

## Skeleton

| Step | Content |
|---|---|
| Claim | Although an industry does create employment, the benefit is weaker than it first appears |
| Reason | "Job creation" is often cited as an unqualified benefit, but the claim only counts quantity — it says nothing about job security, pay, or working conditions |
| Mechanism | The jobs tend to be precarious/seasonal/low-paid, which is where the real quality-of-life impact lies |
| Impact | The benefit of "job creation" is weaker than the headline number suggests |
| Example | The tourism/hospitality industry creates many jobs, but they are often seasonal, with long periods of the year with no work, and low wages for frontline workers |

## Reusable IELTS Angle
Standard rebuttal to the simplified argument "this brings jobs, so it's good" —
widely applicable whenever an essay claims a positive from employment growth alone.

## Speaking Use
Part 3 answers to "Do you think [industry/policy] benefits workers?": "It does create
jobs, but I'm not sure the jobs are always good ones — a lot of them are seasonal or
low-paid, so the benefit isn't as strong as the headline number suggests."

## Useful Chunks
- "precarious / seasonal / low-paid"
- "the benefit is weaker than it first appears"
- "long periods of the year with no work"
