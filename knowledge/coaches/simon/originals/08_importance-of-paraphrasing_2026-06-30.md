# IELTS Advice: The importance of paraphrasing

> Source: Simon (IELTS Simon)
> Saved: 2026-06-30

Here's a theory that I have: Your ability to paraphrase is the best indicator of your level of English.
In fact, when I taught English at Manchester University, I used to use 'paraphrasing tests' as a quick way to check my students' English. If I had a new class of students, I would ask them to suggest alternative ways of expressing a simple idea. For example, I might ask my students to paraphrase this sentence:
The lesson was interesting.
A beginner or intermediate student would struggle to offer alternatives, but a more advanced student should be able to use the language in a more flexible way. For example:
The lesson was fascinating / engaging / absorbing / thought-provoking…
It was a fascinating (engaging etc.) lesson.
It was an interesting class / lecture / seminar / tutorial / session…
I found the class interesting / engaging etc.
The teacher made me think / gave me food for thought / raised some interesting ideas etc.
Can you see how the ability to paraphrase is directly linked to your level of English? The more English you know, the more alternatives you will be able to suggest.
The vocabulary score
If you've seen my lessons about the band descriptors, you'll remember the key phrases in the vocabulary column of the descriptor table:
range of vocabulary
flexibility
precision
less common lexical items
awareness of style and collocation
If you are good at paraphrasing, you'll demonstrate all of the criteria above. You'll be able to avoid repetition and express your ideas with flexibility and precision.
Synonyms and collocation
Let's have a look at the idea of 'awareness of style and collocation'.
When paraphrasing the sentence "the lesson was interesting" (above), I chose the words fascinating, engaging, absorbing and thought-provoking. These synonyms all collocate with (work well with) the word 'lesson'.
However, not all synonyms of the word 'interesting' are appropriate when we're describing a lesson. For example, the following synonyms (from my dictionary) don't really work here:
– entrancing
– appealing
– attractive
– enthralling (too exaggerated)
This is where students often go wrong, especially when they rely on their dictionaries. The sentence "The lesson was attractive" doesn't work, and a native speaker wouldn't say this.
So, a big part of the paraphrasing skill is knowing which words work in the context to create appropriate collocations.
Whereas a beginner or intermediate student will trust the dictionary, an advanced user of English will know which synonyms work and which don't. This is awareness of collocation.
