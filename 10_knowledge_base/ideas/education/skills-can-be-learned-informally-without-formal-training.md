# Idea: Skills can be learned informally without formal training

## Theme
education

## Learning Status
New

## Core Idea
Many important skills have historically been, and still can be, learned through
informal means — observation, family/community transmission, and trial and error —
rather than requiring a formal course or qualification.

## Skeleton

| Step | Content |
|---|---|
| Claim | Formal training is not the only, or even the primary, way people acquire a given skill |
| Reason | People have been acquiring this skill successfully for a long time through non-formal means, which suggests formal instruction isn't the necessary ingredient |
| Mechanism | Skills like this are built through repeated real exposure — observing others, trying things out, making mistakes, adjusting — which teaches judgement in a way a classroom course struggles to replicate |
| Impact | This weakens the case for making formal training compulsory, since the skill is already being transmitted effectively without it |
| Example | Parenting skills: learned from family, friends, neighbours, and culture, and through trial and error with one's own children — for example, trying different strategies with a badly-behaved child and gradually learning what works |

## Reusable IELTS Angle
Rebuttal to any essay proposing formal courses/training/certification as necessary
for a skill that is traditionally learned informally — pairs well with [[mandatory-proposals-often-fail-on-enforcement]] when the question proposes a compulsory course.

## Speaking Use
Part 3 answers to "Do you think people need formal training to [do X]?": "Not
necessarily — a lot of these skills are things people pick up naturally, through
experience and trial and error, rather than in a classroom."
