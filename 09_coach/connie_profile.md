## 5. Writing Profile

### 5.1 Overall

**Current estimate: Band 5.0–5.5**

---

### 5.2 Task Response — Critical

*5.2.1 Misreading the question type*
- `agree/disagree` ≠ `positive/negative` ≠ `discuss both views`
- Two-part questions: must answer both parts, not just one
- Position must be stated clearly in the introduction — not buried at the end
- Paraphrasing cannot change intensity or meaning: `nobody` ≠ `fewer people`
- Questions with `"because + premise"`: must respond directly to the premise
  - Technique: acknowledge premise has partial validity → argue why your position outweighs it

*5.2.2 Underdeveloped ideas*
- Pattern: Point → stop (missing explanation, cause, consequence, example, result)
- Required pattern: **Point → Explain → Example → Result**
- Never end a paragraph immediately after stating the main point

---

### 5.3 Coherence & Cohesion — High

*5.3.1 Weak logical development*
- Ideas are individually understandable but not logically connected
- Common: abrupt transitions, insufficient explanation between ideas
- After every sentence, ask: "Why?" / "How?" / "Can I add one more sentence?"

*5.3.2 Unequal paragraph weight*
- Some body paragraphs much shorter than others — essays feel unbalanced
- Aim for similar development across both body paragraphs
- If one paragraph is overloaded: "Does any content here belong to a different bullet?" — move it

*5.3.3 Connector accuracy*
- Repeated errors with: `while`, `whereas`, `however`, `although`, `despite`, `on the other hand`
- Pattern: connector is grammatically wrong or does not express the intended logical relationship
- Study connectors by function, not by memorisation

*Discussion Essay specific:*
- Body 1 (weaker side) is too superficial — must argue the opposing view fairly, as if you believe it

### 6.2 Reading Weakness — Matching Features

Priority: High

Diagnosis:

Can locate related words, but answer verification is not strict enough.

Main weakness:

Not finding the answer, but confirming whether the answer fully satisfies the statement.

Common problems:

* grabs keywords too quickly
* sentence core is processed slowly
* understands local words but not full logic
* treats related words as paraphrase
* fills gaps using common sense or inference
* does not check all conditions in the statement

Key rule: `related words ≠ paraphrase`

Training focus:

* split each statement into 2–4 conditions
* verify all conditions against the text
* check scope: person · method · period · location · category
* confirm whether evidence is direct or inferred
* slow down on: `not` · `least` · `most` · `generally` · `preferred` · `more` · `less`

SOP:

1. Read statements first. Identify keywords (names, places, dates, numbers, technical terms).
2. Read feature list (options).
3. Scan passage to locate likely areas. Build a paragraph concept map.
4. Read the relevant sentence carefully. Match full meaning, not just keywords.
5. If not found after two scans, mark and move on.

Stopping rule: two scan attempts maximum — no third scan during first pass.

---

### 6.3 Reading Weakness — True / False / Not Given

Priority: Medium

Key issue: wording must be compared strictly with the passage.

Rules:
* True = clearly supported by passage
* False = passage says the opposite
* Not Given = passage does not mention it at all (not the same as False)

Training focus:
* avoid relying on memory or inference
* check absolute words: `always` · `never` · `all` · `only`
* verify the exact claim, not just topic similarity

---

### 6.4 Reading Weakness — Short Answer / Completion

Priority: Medium

Main risks: word limit · spelling · singular/plural · wrong word form · grammar fit

Rules:
* copy answers exactly from passage — do not paraphrase
* check word limit every time
* determine likely part of speech before searching

### 7.3 Listening Part 2 — Matching SOP

Before audio:

1. Read question stem: what is the task asking? (feature / problem / recommendation / limitation / evaluation / suitable audience)
2. Label options — do not memorise full sentences.
3. Predict paraphrases for each label.

During audio:

1. Track the item name to know which question you are on.
2. Listen for descriptions, not just keywords.
3. Watch for reversal signals: `but` · `however` · `actually` · `in fact` · `although` · `not anymore` · `used to` · `originally` · `now` · `instead`
4. Choose when 70% sure. Maximum 2 seconds hesitation. Move on immediately if uncertain.

After audio:

1. Check missed answers.
2. Confirm letters copied correctly.
3. Check word limit, spelling, singular/plural.

### 10.1 Memory / Internalisation Style (added 2026-07-04)

Plain-text memorisation (copying, re-reading, rote recall) tends to get stuck at
**recognition** for Connie — she can understand a pattern on sight but can't
retrieve it under exam pressure. This isn't an age issue; it's a match issue between
the input method and how she processes language. She responds better to:
image/visual scene → gesture/embodied action → spoken output → written form last.
This plays to her actual strengths — understanding, creativity, situational sense —
rather than rote storage. ⚠️ The written-up practice method (`practice_method.md`)
