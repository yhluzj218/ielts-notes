# CATEGORY 1 — Place & Architecture

---

**Front:**
A building looks new, uses current design or technology, and feels up-to-date.
What adjective describes this?

**Back:**
**Modern**
Pronunciation: /ˈmɑːdərn/ · MOD-ern
Form: adjective
Related forms:
modernise = verb
modernisation = noun
Example:
The city centre has several modern office buildings with glass facades.
Note:
One of the most useful IELTS words for describing buildings, cities, and technology. Usually positive — suggests efficiency and contemporary style.
Common collocations:
modern building · modern design · modern city · modern architecture · modern approach

---

**Front:**
A building or area has large rooms or open areas with plenty of room to move around.
What adjective describes this?

**Back:**
**Spacious**
Pronunciation: /ˈspeɪʃəs/ · SPAY-shus
Form: adjective
Related forms:
space = noun
spaciously = adverb (rare)
Example:
The new library is spacious and well-lit, making it a comfortable place to study.
Note:
Commonly used in IELTS Speaking Part 2 to describe homes, schools, or public spaces. Positive connotation.
Common collocations:
spacious room · spacious apartment · spacious garden · spacious interior

---

**Front:**
A building is old and in very bad condition because it has not been repaired or maintained.
What adjective describes this?

**Back:**
**Dilapidated**
Pronunciation: /dɪˈlæpɪdeɪtɪd/ · di-LAP-i-day-tid
Form: adjective
Related forms:
dilapidation = noun
Example:
Several dilapidated houses near the old factory have been left empty for years.
Note:
A strong Band 7+ word for describing run-down buildings. More specific than "old" — implies neglect and structural decay.
Common collocations:
dilapidated building · dilapidated house · dilapidated structure · dilapidated area

---

**Front:**
A place is important because of its connection to the past, famous events, or long history.
What adjective describes this?

**Back:**
**Historic**
Pronunciation: /hɪˈstɔːrɪk/ · his-TOR-ik
Form: adjective
Related forms:
history = noun
historical = adjective (means "relating to history"; historic = "important in history")
Example:
The government has invested in restoring the city's historic buildings.
Note:
⚠️ historic ≠ historical. Historic = famous or important in history. Historical = relating to history in general.
Common collocations:
historic building · historic site · historic centre · historic significance · historic town

---

**Front:**
A building or neighbourhood is in poor condition — neglected, tired-looking, and below its former standard.
What adjective (two words, hyphenated) describes this?

**Back:**
**Run-down**
Pronunciation: /ˈrʌndaʊn/ · RUN-down
Form: adjective (compound)
Related forms:
No common verb form. Use "fall into disrepair" to describe the process.
Example:
The run-down estate on the edge of town was finally scheduled for redevelopment.
Note:
More informal than "dilapidated." Common in speaking. Both describe poor physical condition, but run-down also implies social/economic neglect.
Common collocations:
run-down area · run-down building · run-down neighbourhood · run-down estate

# CATEGORY 2 — Society & Community

---

**Front:**
A society includes many different groups of people — different cultures, backgrounds, beliefs, or identities.
What noun describes this variety?

**Back:**
**Diversity**
Pronunciation: /daɪˈvɜːrsɪti/ · dy-VUR-si-tee
Form: noun
Related forms:
diverse = adjective
diversify = verb
Example:
Cities with greater cultural diversity tend to be more creative and innovative.
Note:
Very common in IELTS Task 2 essays on society, culture, education, and immigration. Often paired with "cultural" or "linguistic."
Common collocations:
cultural diversity · linguistic diversity · diversity of opinion · embrace diversity · celebrate diversity

---

**Front:**
Something — a tradition, language, or way of life — has been passed down from previous generations and forms part of a group's identity.
What noun describes this?

**Back:**
**Heritage**
Pronunciation: /ˈherɪtɪdʒ/ · HER-i-tij
Form: noun (uncountable)
Related forms:
No verb form. Use "preserve / protect / pass down heritage."
Example:
Losing a language means losing a community's cultural heritage forever.
Note:
Often used with "cultural," "linguistic," or "national." Frequently appears in IELTS essays on language, tradition, and globalisation.
Common collocations:
cultural heritage · linguistic heritage · national heritage · heritage site · preserve heritage

---

**Front:**
Something relates to the relationships or communication between people — especially in social or professional situations.
What adjective describes this?

**Back:**
**Interpersonal**
Pronunciation: /ˌɪntərˈpɜːrsənəl/ · in-ter-PUR-son-ul
Form: adjective
Related forms:
No common noun form alone. Use "interpersonal skills" as a phrase.
Example:
Schools should focus on developing students' interpersonal skills, not just academic knowledge.
Note:
Almost always appears in the phrase "interpersonal skills." Useful in education and work-related essays.
Common collocations:
interpersonal skills · interpersonal relationships · interpersonal communication · interpersonal conflict

---

**Front:**
An activity, place, or experience used to belong almost exclusively to one specific group (often a wealthy or privileged one), before becoming available to more people.
What phrase describes "used to belong exclusively to [group]"?

**Back:**
**The preserve of [a group]**
Pronunciation: /ðə prɪˈzɜːrv əv/
Form: noun phrase
Related forms:
preserve = noun (exclusive domain) — different from the verb "to preserve" (protect/keep)
Example:
International travel used to be the preserve of the wealthy classes, but budget airlines have made it accessible to almost everyone.
Note:
Natural way to set up a "then vs now" contrast — something exclusive in the past becoming widely accessible now. From International Tourism model essay (2026-07-02).
Common collocations:
the preserve of the wealthy · the preserve of the elite · once the preserve of [group]

---

**Front:**
The ability of people or things to move or travel — including how easily people can relocate, travel abroad, or change social/economic position.
What noun describes this?

**Back:**
**Mobility**
Pronunciation: /moʊˈbɪlɪti/ · moh-BIL-i-tee
Form: noun (uncountable)
Related forms:
mobile = adjective
No common verb form — use "move" or "travel."
Example:
This level of mobility has brought both cultural benefits and environmental costs.
Note:
Useful in essays on travel, globalisation, and social class ("social mobility"). From International Tourism model essay (2026-07-02).
Common collocations:
international mobility · social mobility · this level of mobility · increased mobility

# CATEGORY 3 — Education & Skills

---

**Front:**
Someone is very skilled at doing something — they have learned it well and do it with ease.
What adjective describes this person?

**Back:**
**Adept**
Pronunciation: /əˈdept/ · uh-DEPT
Form: adjective
Related forms:
No common noun or verb form. Use "adept at" + verb-ing.
Example:
Young people are so adept at using websites and search engines that they can find information independently.
Note:
Always followed by "at." More formal and precise than "good at." Useful in education, technology, and skills essays.
Common collocations:
adept at using · adept at navigating · adept at solving · become adept at

---

**Front:**
Someone has the skills and ability needed to do something well in a particular area.
What noun describes this quality?

**Back:**
**Competence**
Pronunciation: /ˈkɒmpɪtəns/ · KOM-pi-tuns
Form: noun (uncountable)
Related forms:
competent = adjective
competently = adverb
Example:
Competence in digital skills is now essential for most jobs in the modern economy.
Note:
Use "competence in [area]" — not "competence of." A more formal alternative to "ability" or "skill."
Common collocations:
competence in · professional competence · technical competence · demonstrate competence · linguistic competence

---

**Front:**
You decide that something is more important than other things and deal with it first.
What verb describes this action?

**Back:**
**Prioritise**
Pronunciation: /praɪˈɒrɪtaɪz/ · pry-OR-i-tyz
Form: verb (British spelling: prioritise / American: prioritize)
Related forms:
priority = noun
Example:
Schools should prioritise critical thinking and social skills over rote memorisation of facts.
Note:
Frequently appears in IELTS essays on education, health, and government policy. Use it when you want to say something deserves more attention than something else.
Common collocations:
prioritise skills · prioritise health · prioritise education · prioritise the needs of

---

**Front:**
Information, resources, or services are easy to get to, find, or use — especially online.
What adjective describes this?

**Back:**
**Accessible**
Pronunciation: /əkˈsesɪbəl/ · ak-SES-i-bul
Form: adjective
Related forms:
access = noun/verb
accessibility = noun
inaccessible = opposite adjective
Example:
As information becomes more accessible online, the role of teachers is changing.
Note:
Very useful in technology and education essays. The phrase "accessible online" appears naturally in discussions about the internet.
Common collocations:
accessible online · freely accessible · accessible to all · accessible information · widely accessible

---

**Front:**
Something you must learn or complete first, before you can move on to more advanced study or tasks.
What noun describes this?

**Back:**
**Prerequisite**
Pronunciation: /ˌpriːˈrekwəzɪt/ · pree-REK-wuh-zit
Form: noun (also adjective)
Related forms:
require = verb
requirement = noun (general need, not necessarily "first")
Example:
A solid understanding of mathematics is a prerequisite for most science subjects.
Note:
Followed by "for" (a prerequisite FOR physics). Precise academic word for education and skills essays — stronger than "necessary first step."
Common collocations:
a prerequisite for · an essential prerequisite · prerequisites for entry · a prerequisite to success

---

**Front:**
A firm, solid understanding of a subject or concept — you really "hold" the idea in your mind.
What noun describes this understanding?

**Back:**
**Grasp**
Pronunciation: /ɡræsp/ · GRASP
Form: noun (also verb = to understand / to seize)
Related forms:
grasp = verb (grasp a concept)
Example:
Students with a strong grasp of mathematics tend to comprehend physics more easily.
Note:
Almost always "a strong/good/solid grasp OF something." Natural replacement for "good understanding" in education essays.
Common collocations:
a strong grasp of · a solid grasp of · grasp a concept · beyond one's grasp

# CATEGORY 4 — Technology & Media

---

**Front:**
Content uses a combination of different formats — such as video, audio, images, and text.
What adjective describes this content?

**Back:**
**Multimedia**
Pronunciation: /ˌmʌltiˈmiːdiə/ · mul-ti-MEE-dee-uh
Form: adjective / noun
Related forms:
No single-word verb form. Use "create multimedia content."
Example:
Many lessons are now available in multimedia format on platforms like YouTube.
Note:
Often used in education and technology essays. A precise word that replaces "videos and other content."
Common collocations:
multimedia format · multimedia content · multimedia platform · multimedia resources · multimedia lessons

---

**Front:**
A digital barrier that requires users to pay before they can read or access online content.
What noun describes this barrier?

**Back:**
**Paywall**
Pronunciation: /ˈpeɪwɔːl/ · PAY-wawl
Form: noun
Related forms:
No verb form. Use "place content behind a paywall."
Example:
Many major newspapers have placed their articles behind a paywall, proving that online content is not always free.
Note:
Specific and modern — demonstrates awareness of how the media industry works. Useful in essays about journalism, media, and the internet.
Common collocations:
behind a paywall · introduce a paywall · paywall model · remove the paywall

---

**Front:**
Money that a company or organisation earns from its business activities — such as advertising or subscriptions.
What noun describes this income?

**Back:**
**Revenue**
Pronunciation: /ˈrevənjuː/ · REV-uh-nyoo
Form: noun (uncountable)
Related forms:
No common verb form. Use "generate / earn revenue."
Example:
Some websites rely on advertising revenue instead of charging users directly.
Note:
More formal and precise than "money" or "income" in an essay context. Common in economy, business, and media essays.
Common collocations:
advertising revenue · generate revenue · revenue stream · annual revenue · tax revenue

# CATEGORY 5 — Economy & Work

---

**Front:**
Something is wanted by many companies or employers and is therefore very valuable in the job market.
What phrase describes this?

**Back:**
**In high demand**
Pronunciation: /ɪn haɪ dɪˈmɑːnd/
Form: adjective phrase
Related forms:
demand = noun/verb · in demand = shorter form
Example:
Competence in programming and data analysis is in high demand in today's job market.
Note:
Very natural-sounding in IELTS essays on work, education, and economy. More specific than "very useful" or "very important."
Common collocations:
in high demand · in great demand · highly sought after · in demand among employers

---

**Front:**
The process of discussing something with another person or group in order to reach an agreement.
What noun describes this process?

**Back:**
**Negotiation**
Pronunciation: /nɪˌɡoʊʃiˈeɪʃən/ · ni-goh-shee-AY-shun
Form: noun
Related forms:
negotiate = verb
negotiator = noun (person)
Example:
Group projects teach students skills such as teamwork, negotiation, and the delegation of tasks.
Note:
Commonly grouped with "teamwork" and "delegation" in essays about education and the workplace. Suggests mature social skills.
Common collocations:
negotiation skills · enter into negotiation · reach through negotiation · salary negotiation

---

**Front:**
The process of assigning tasks or responsibilities to other people in a group or organisation.
What noun describes this?

**Back:**
**Delegation**
Pronunciation: /ˌdelɪˈɡeɪʃən/ · del-i-GAY-shun
Form: noun
Related forms:
delegate = verb / noun (a person who represents a group)
Example:
Effective leadership requires clear delegation of tasks and strong communication.
Note:
In IELTS essays, often appears with "negotiation" and "teamwork" as examples of social or workplace skills.
Common collocations:
delegation of tasks · effective delegation · delegation of authority · team delegation

---

**Front:**
A job, income, or way of life is uncertain and insecure — it could disappear or fail at any time, often because it depends on seasonal or unstable conditions.
What adjective describes this?

**Back:**
**Precarious**
Pronunciation: /prɪˈkeəriəs/ · pri-KAIR-ee-us
Form: adjective
Related forms:
precariously = adverb
precariousness = noun
Example:
Although the hospitality industry brings employment, it tends to be precarious because much of the work is seasonal.
Note:
Precise, natural word for describing unstable jobs or income — stronger and more specific than "unstable." From International Tourism model essay (2026-07-02).
Common collocations:
precarious employment · a precarious existence · a precarious position · precarious income

---

**Front:**
A large company that operates and owns businesses in more than one country.
What phrase describes this kind of company?

**Back:**
**Multinational corporation(s)**
Pronunciation: /ˌmʌltiˈnæʃənəl kɔːrpəˈreɪʃən/
Form: noun phrase
Related forms:
multinational = adjective (can also stand alone: "a multinational")
Example:
Profits from tourism are often taken out of the local economy by multinational corporations.
Note:
Common in essays about globalisation, tourism, and economic inequality — pairs naturally with "profits taken out of" or "local economy." From International Tourism model essay (2026-07-02).
Common collocations:
multinational corporations · large multinational companies · profits taken out by multinational corporations

# CATEGORY 6 — Language & Culture

---

**Front:**
Something relates to language — its structure, history, or cultural role.
What adjective describes this?

**Back:**
**Linguistic**
Pronunciation: /lɪŋˈɡwɪstɪk/ · ling-GWIS-tik
Form: adjective
Related forms:
linguistics = noun (the study of language)
linguist = noun (a person)
Example:
The linguistic diversity of a society reflects its rich cultural history.
Note:
Precise and academic — appropriate for essays on language, culture, and globalisation. Often paired with "diversity" or "heritage."
Common collocations:
linguistic diversity · linguistic heritage · linguistic skills · linguistic knowledge · linguistic community

---

**Front:**
A species, language, or tradition no longer exists — it has completely disappeared from the world.
What adjective describes this?

**Back:**
**Extinct**
Pronunciation: /ɪkˈstɪŋkt/ · ik-STINGKT
Form: adjective
Related forms:
extinction = noun
become extinct = verb phrase
Example:
Without active preservation efforts, many minority languages will become extinct within a generation.
Note:
Usually used for species (animals, plants) but increasingly used for languages in IELTS essays. Stronger than "disappear" — suggests permanent, total loss.
Common collocations:
become extinct · extinct language · extinct species · face extinction · on the verge of extinction

---

**Front:**
You give new life, energy, or importance to something that was declining or losing relevance.
What verb describes this action?

**Back:**
**Revitalise**
Pronunciation: /riːˈvaɪtəlaɪz/ · ree-VY-tuh-lyz
Form: verb (British: revitalise / American: revitalize)
Related forms:
revitalisation = noun
Example:
Governments should invest in programmes to revitalise endangered languages before they are lost forever.
Note:
More active and positive than "preserve" or "save." Implies restoration and renewal, not just protection.
Common collocations:
revitalise a language · revitalise a community · revitalise an economy · revitalise interest in

---

**Front:**
Something gradually wears away, weakens, or disappears over time — often due to external forces or neglect.
What noun describes this gradual loss?

**Back:**
**Erosion**
Pronunciation: /ɪˈroʊʒən/ · i-ROH-zhun
Form: noun (uncountable)
Related forms:
erode = verb
Example:
The erosion of minority languages is accelerating in an increasingly globalised world.
Note:
Originally a geological term (soil erosion). Used metaphorically in IELTS essays to describe the slow loss of culture, rights, or traditions. Stronger and more vivid than "loss" or "decline."
Common collocations:
cultural erosion · erosion of identity · erosion of values · gradual erosion · language erosion

# CATEGORY 7 — Change & Progress

---

**Front:**
A movement or change away from one approach, system, or idea and towards a new one.
What noun describes this change in direction?

**Back:**
**Shift**
Pronunciation: /ʃɪft/ · SHIFT
Form: noun / verb
Related forms:
shift (verb) = to move or change
Example:
A shift away from traditional teaching methods would allow schools to develop students' practical skills.
Note:
Very common in IELTS essays on social change, policy, and education. The phrase "a shift away from X" is especially useful when arguing for change.
Common collocations:
a shift away from · a shift towards · a significant shift · a cultural shift · a policy shift

---

**Front:**
One thing is heavier, more important, or more significant than another thing — and wins the comparison.
What verb describes this?

**Back:**
**Outweigh**
Pronunciation: /ˌaʊtˈweɪ/ · owt-WAY
Form: verb
Related forms:
No noun form. Use "the benefits outweigh the drawbacks."
Example:
The long-term benefits of preserving endangered languages far outweigh the cost involved.
Note:
Essential for IELTS argument essays. Used to compare two things and say one is more significant. Especially useful in conclusions or when conceding a point.
Common collocations:
outweigh the benefits · outweigh the risks · outweigh the costs · far outweigh · clearly outweigh

---

**Front:**
Something becomes smaller, less important, or less powerful — gradually losing its strength or value.
What verb describes this?

**Back:**
**Diminish**
Pronunciation: /dɪˈmɪnɪʃ/ · di-MIN-ish
Form: verb
Related forms:
diminished = adjective
diminishing = adjective
Example:
Without government support, the role of community languages will continue to diminish.
Note:
More formal and gradual than "reduce" or "decrease." Suggests a slow, ongoing process.
Common collocations:
diminish in importance · diminish over time · diminishing returns · a diminished role · slowly diminish

# CATEGORY 8 — Personal Development

---

**Front:**
Someone has the natural ability or capacity to achieve great things in the future — if given the opportunity.
What noun describes this?

**Back:**
**Potential**
Pronunciation: /pəˈtenʃəl/ · puh-TEN-shul
Form: noun / adjective
Related forms:
potentially = adverb
Example:
A skills-based education helps all children, regardless of academic ability, to reach their potential.
Note:
"Reach one's potential" and "fulfil one's potential" are both natural collocations. Common in education, work, and personal development essays.
Common collocations:
reach their potential · fulfil potential · unlock potential · potential for growth · show potential

---

**Front:**
The ability to guide, inspire, and take responsibility for a group of people — especially in work or community settings.
What noun describes this quality?

**Back:**
**Leadership**
Pronunciation: /ˈliːdərʃɪp/ · LEE-der-ship
Form: noun (uncountable)
Related forms:
lead = verb
leader = noun
Example:
Group projects in schools can develop students' leadership qualities and emotional intelligence.
Note:
Often paired with "qualities" in IELTS essays. Useful in education, work, and society topics.
Common collocations:
leadership skills · leadership qualities · strong leadership · develop leadership · leadership role

---

**Front:**
The ability to understand and manage your own emotions, and to be sensitive to the emotions of others.
What phrase describes this capacity?

**Back:**
**Emotional intelligence**
Pronunciation: /ɪˈmoʊʃənəl ɪnˈtelɪdʒəns/ · i-MOH-shun-ul in-TEL-i-juns
Form: noun phrase (uncountable)
Related forms:
emotionally intelligent = adjective phrase
Example:
Teaching social skills in schools helps develop students' emotional intelligence and empathy.
Note:
A modern, widely recognised concept in education and workplace essays. More specific and impressive than "understanding feelings."
Common collocations:
develop emotional intelligence · emotional intelligence skills · high emotional intelligence · lack of emotional intelligence

---

**Front:**
Someone becomes so exhausted and demotivated — usually from prolonged stress or forced effort — that they can no longer continue.
What phrasal verb describes this?

**Back:**
**Burn out**
Pronunciation: /ˌbɜːrn ˈaʊt/ · burn-OUT
Form: phrasal verb (noun form: burnout, uncountable)
Related forms:
burnout = noun (suffer from burnout)
burnt out / burned out = adjective
Example:
Students who are forced to study subjects they have no interest in are likely to burn out and give up.
Note:
Modern, natural word for motivation/pressure topics (education, work). The noun "burnout" is also common: "employee burnout."
Common collocations:
burn out quickly · likely to burn out · suffer from burnout · avoid burnout

---

**Front:**
A person or thing develops quickly and successfully — they thrive, especially after conditions improve.
What verb describes this?

**Back:**
**Flourish**
Pronunciation: /ˈflɜːrɪʃ/ · FLUR-ish
Form: verb
Related forms:
flourishing = adjective
Example:
She struggled through compulsory chemistry but flourished once she switched to design.
Note:
Strongest in before/after contrasts — pair it with "struggle" for effect. Works for people, businesses, cities, and cultures.
Common collocations:
flourish in a new environment · begin to flourish · businesses flourish · allow talent to flourish

---

**Front:**
The satisfying feeling you get after achieving something you worked hard for.
What fixed phrase describes this feeling?

**Back:**
**A sense of accomplishment**
Pronunciation: /əˈkʌmplɪʃmənt/ · uh-KUM-plish-munt
Form: fixed noun phrase
Related forms:
accomplish = verb
accomplished = adjective (skilled)
Example:
Completing a project of their own choosing gives students a profound and long-lasting sense of accomplishment.
Note:
The natural collocation for "成就感" — do not translate as "wellness" or "achievement feeling." Common in education, work, and personal development essays.
Common collocations:
a sense of accomplishment · a profound sense of accomplishment · gives me a sense of accomplishment

# CATEGORY 9 — Argument & Academic Writing

---

**Front:**
Something is absolutely necessary — there is no choice but to do it.
What adjective describes this level of necessity?

**Back:**
**Imperative**
Pronunciation: /ɪmˈperətɪv/ · im-PAIR-uh-tiv
Form: adjective / noun
Related forms:
imperatively = adverb (rare)
Example:
It is imperative that schools adapt their teaching methods to meet the demands of modern society.
Note:
Stronger than "necessary" or "important." Use it when you want to say something must happen without any compromise.
Common collocations:
it is imperative that · imperative to act · an imperative · moral imperative · consider it imperative

---

**Front:**
You do something very carefully, with great effort and attention to detail — even if it takes a long time.
What adverb describes this way of working?

**Back:**
**Painstakingly**
Pronunciation: /ˈpeɪnzteɪkɪŋli/ · PAYNZ-tay-king-lee
Form: adverb
Related forms:
painstaking = adjective
Example:
Without scholars painstakingly decoding the ancient scripts, the knowledge inscribed on temple walls would have been lost forever.
Note:
Used to emphasise the difficulty or effort of a task. Makes examples feel more vivid and specific.
Common collocations:
painstakingly documented · painstakingly restored · painstakingly decoded · painstakingly collected · painstaking research

---

**Front:**
You make an exact copy of something — reproduce it precisely so it works the same way.
What verb describes this action?

**Back:**
**Replicate**
Pronunciation: /ˈreplɪkeɪt/ · REP-li-kayt
Form: verb
Related forms:
replication = noun
Example:
No single language, however widely spoken, could fully replicate the precision of a minority language.
Note:
More precise than "copy" — implies faithfulness to the original. Common in science, research, and cultural essays.
Common collocations:
replicate the results · replicate success · replicate a model · cannot be replicated · difficult to replicate

---

**Front:**
A rule or requirement that everyone must follow — there is no choice about whether to do it.
What adjective describes this?

**Back:**
**Compulsory**
Pronunciation: /kəmˈpʌlsəri/ · kum-PUL-suh-ree
Form: adjective
Related forms:
compel = verb
compulsion = noun
Example:
Making parenting courses compulsory raises the question of how non-attendance would be enforced.
Note:
Useful whenever an essay discusses a proposal to require something by law or policy — pairs naturally with words like "enforce" and "mandatory."
Common collocations:
compulsory attendance · compulsory training · compulsory education · make [X] compulsory

---

**Front:**
A plan or idea cannot actually work when you try to apply it in real life, even if it sounds reasonable in theory.
What adjective describes this?

**Back:**
**Impractical**
Pronunciation: /ɪmˈpræktɪkəl/ · im-PRAK-ti-kul
Form: adjective
Related forms:
practical = adjective (opposite)
impracticality = noun
Example:
Requiring every parent to attend a class is impractical, since many have jobs and busy schedules.
Note:
The standard word for rebutting a proposal on the grounds of real-world feasibility, not principle. Common in Agree/Disagree essays that concede an idea sounds good but argue it can't work.
Common collocations:
completely impractical · prove impractical · an impractical solution · impractical to enforce

---

**Front:**
Official or outside involvement in a private or personal matter, especially when unwanted.
What noun describes this?

**Back:**
**Intervention**
Pronunciation: /ˌɪntərˈvenʃən/ · in-tur-VEN-shun
Form: noun
Related forms:
intervene = verb
interventionist = adjective
Example:
Parenting skills are usually learned without any official intervention, through family, culture, and experience.
Note:
Useful when arguing that government/official involvement in a personal area is unnecessary or unwelcome — common in family, education, and government-society topics.
Common collocations:
official intervention · government intervention · without intervention · require intervention

# CATEGORY 10 — Environment & Sustainability

---

**Front:**
Resources or systems are used in a way that can continue for a long time without causing harm to the environment or future generations.
What adjective describes this?

**Back:**
**Sustainable**
Pronunciation: /səˈsteɪnəbəl/ · sus-TAY-nuh-bul
Form: adjective
Related forms:
sustainability = noun
sustainably = adverb
sustain = verb
Example:
Governments must develop sustainable energy solutions to reduce reliance on fossil fuels.
Note:
【筆記外補充】Not from model essays — included because it is one of the most common IELTS Task 2 words across environment, economy, and policy topics.
Common collocations:
sustainable development · sustainable energy · sustainable future · environmentally sustainable · sustainable solution

**Front:**
Something is used up or slowly destroyed — especially a natural resource or a person's energy or patience.
What verb describes this gradual using up?

**Back:**
**Deplete**
Pronunciation: /dɪˈpliːt/ · di-PLEET
Form: verb
Related forms:
depletion = noun
depleted = adjective
Example:
Natural resources are being depleted at an alarming rate due to overconsumption.
Note:
【筆記外補充】Not from model essays — included because it is high-frequency in IELTS environment essays. More specific than "use up" or "reduce."
Common collocations:
deplete natural resources · ozone depletion · rapidly depleted · deplete energy reserves · resource depletion

**Front:**
Serious harm or suffering is caused to something or someone — often used to describe the negative consequences of an action on the environment, society, or people.
What phrase describes "damage caused to [X]"?

**Back:**
**Damage inflicted on [X]**
Pronunciation: /ˈdæmɪdʒ ɪnˈflɪktɪd ɒn/
Form: verb phrase (inflict = verb)
Related forms:
inflict = verb (to cause something unpleasant to be experienced)
infliction = noun (rare)
Example:
The damage inflicted on the environment by mass tourism cannot be justified by its economic benefits.
Note:
Stronger and more formal than "damage caused to" — useful in conclusion sentences that weigh costs against benefits. From International Tourism model essay (2026-07-02).
Common collocations:
damage inflicted on the environment · inflict harm on · inflict damage · inflict suffering
