## Teaching Philosophy

IELTS Speaking is a test of natural communication, not a performance of learned techniques. The examiner is assessing whether you can convey information clearly and naturally — not whether you use specific frameworks, complex vocabulary, or memorised structures.

> "Don't overthink."

> "It's got to be your communication during the exam, not imitating a native speaker."

The goal is to transmit a large amount of natural information. Simple language that communicates clearly scores higher than complex language that does not.

## Core Principles

### Part 1

- Answer the question directly.
- **Stop when you have answered.** Do not extend into unrequested topics.
- Answer length follows question type: factual / location questions → short; `why` / opinion questions → longer.
- Avoid narrowing your thinking to a single angle — give 2–3 different perspectives quickly.

### Part 2

- Fill 2 minutes. Stopping early is worse than running over — the examiner will stop you at 2 minutes anyway.
- **Use the question card itself as the structure.** No STAR, no special frameworks needed.
  - Opening = answer the first question on the card
  - Middle = answer the middle questions
  - Closing = answer the final question (usually a feelings/reflection question)
- Use the 1-minute preparation time to **organise the order of what you want to say** — not to write a script. Think, rank, then speak.
- If a topic has high vocabulary demands, it is acceptable to shift to a more manageable angle during prep time (e.g. if asked about a TV show, choose one you can describe easily).
- Useful linking phrases for Part 2: `"Let me tell you a bit more about it..."`, `"One episode I remember was when..."`

### Part 3

- Answer directly.
- Speak about **society and general people**, not about yourself (that is Part 1 and 2).
- Give longer answers where possible — expand on reasoning.
- Do not reach for complex structures or memorised phrases — the examiner can detect this and it reduces the score.
- Express a genuine opinion; do not overcomplicate the reasoning.

## Recommended Practice Methods

- Self-record Part 2 responses. If the recording is under 2 minutes, restart. Do not stop early.
- Self-question practice: pick a Part 2 topic, ask it to yourself, answer aloud. Joe confirmed this method works.
- Vocabulary: learn words that come up when you are actually stuck in practice — not from a list. Approximately 5 words per session.
- Maintain a consistent daily practice rhythm. Fluency regresses noticeably after one week without practice.

---

## What to Avoid (Joe's explicit instructions)

- **Do not watch IELTS YouTube channels or follow IELTS social media.** Joe is explicit that this content causes scores to drop — it focuses on techniques the examiner does not care about and often teaches incorrect strategies.
  - General English content (podcasts, audiobooks, films) is fine. IELTS-specific content is not.
- Do not memorise answers or prepared scripts — examiners ask unexpected questions deliberately to test spontaneous response.
- Do not use artificial frameworks (STAR etc.) — use the question card structure instead.
- Do not try to imitate native speaker speech patterns — it interferes with natural communication.
